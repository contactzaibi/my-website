Who I Am

The real introduction

3.15CGPA / 4.0

There are two kinds of people who see something broken.

The first kind notes it, reports it, and moves on. The second kind goes home that evening, sits down, and builds the fix.

I found out which kind I am during my second week as an office clerk.

Eight people. One printer. One notebook to track who printed what. A reasonable system — until arguments started. Someone couldn't remember printing forty pages three weeks ago. Someone thought I was overcharging. I had all the data. They had none. A paper notebook wasn't going to fix that.

That evening I sat at home and thought: what if everyone could just see everything?

Two weeks later, PrintDesk was live. Each person had their own login, their own dashboard, their own balance visible on their phone. My manager saw it and said: "Positive step. Keep it simple." I've kept those words since.

I'm not a software engineer. I studied Economics at a government college in Toba Tek Singh, not because it was a calculated career move, but because it was what I had access to, and something about it genuinely caught. The way markets breathe. The way policy ripples into real lives. The way data, read correctly, tells you what's actually happening beneath the surface.

What I've learned — through the tools I've built, the research I've run, the roles I've taken, is that I don't do well with static. I need problems that evolve, questions that compound, work where showing up with a better idea is encouraged rather than just tolerated.

I'm twenty-three. From a small city. I built working software in a government office in my spare time, without being asked.

I'm just getting started.

Final Year Research

A question that started with real data

Pakistani firms that invest in R&D don't automatically innovate. I spent a year trying to understand why.

BS Economics Thesis · 2025–2026

The answer turned out to be less about money and more about people.

The Nexus of R&D, Absorptive Capacity, and Innovation: Evidence from Pakistani Enterprises

Supervisor
Dr. Muhammad Zeeshan Younas
Lecturer, Applied Economics & Research Methods · GCUF
Dataset
World Bank Enterprise Survey 2015
696 Pakistani firms · Stata analysis
Key Finding

R&D investment alone yields statistically insignificant innovation outcomes. Returns become significant only when moderated by employee training (β = 0.130, p < 0.01) and process upgrades (β = 0.230, p < 0.01) human capital and operational readiness are co-requisites, not optional additions.
